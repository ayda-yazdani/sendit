Where it starts

You know how Gen Z friends communicate?
They send each other reels.

Not texts. Not "hey let's hang." They see something — a rooftop bar, a restaurant, a club night, a recipe, a trip idea — and they send it to their friends with no caption, or maybe just a "we should do this." And the friend replies "yes omg" or "🔥" and then absolutely nothing happens.

That reel disappears into the group chat. Buried under memes and voice notes and people asking what time they're meeting. The plan that was genuinely wanted by everyone in that chat just… dies. Every time.

"The problem isn't that we don't know what we want to do. We literally send it to each other every day. The problem is nothing ever converts."

And it's not just event reels either. It's political stuff, brainrot, dark humour, aesthetic videos, throwback content, recipes, music — everything a group sends each other is actually taste data. It's a portrait of who they are as a group. It's just sitting in Instagram DMs doing nothing.

The idea

A shared board where your group
drops reels from anywhere.

One person creates a Sendit board and drops the link into their existing group chat — WhatsApp, Instagram DMs, wherever. Everyone joins in one tap. Then whenever anyone in the group sees something they'd want to do together, instead of sending it into the chat where it gets buried, they share it to the board. Two taps. Same effort as forwarding a reel to someone.

Works from Instagram, TikTok, YouTube Shorts, anywhere — because all Sendit needs is the URL. The platform doesn't matter to the user. They just share.

The AI isn't pulling from an algorithm or a generic recommendations engine. It's reading what this specific group of people specifically chose to send each other. That's a fundamentally different signal — way higher intent than anything passive.

The output
"Three of you have been sending Japanese dinner reels for two weeks. You're all free Saturday at 7. Here's a spot in Shoreditch. Here's what to bring. Who's in?" — not a list of options, one call. No debate, no 200 messages.
The calendar bit

Everyone links their calendar
privately.

Each person in the group connects their Google or Apple Calendar privately. Nobody sees each other's events — not even the group. The app just computes when everyone's free and only suggests plans within those windows. It holds a free/busy mask, not your actual schedule.

The reason it's private is important. Not every friend group is that close. Some groups would never share calendars with each other but still want to find a time they all line up. Private by default means more people actually sign up.

The part that actually makes plans happen

Because "yes we should" isn't
a commitment.

Once a plan is suggested, the board switches to commitment mode. Everyone marks themselves in, maybe, or out. You can see who's actually committed and who's still a maybe. For ticketed events, people can drop a screenshot of their confirmation — a receipt wall showing who's bought their ticket and who hasn't. The empty avatars do the work without anyone having to say anything.

🎟
Commitment board
Everyone marks In / Maybe / Out. Live tally. Social contract, not a poll.
"4 people said in. Tom and Priya still haven't responded. 48hrs left."
🧾
Receipt wall
For ticketed events — drop your confirmation screenshot. Row of avatars, green for confirmed, grey for pending. The empty grey ones do the work.
"Priya ✓ · Mehrdad ✓ · Tom ⬜ · Sofia ⬜"
⏰
The decay reminder
If nobody acts on a suggestion within 72 hours, the app sends one nudge. After that it archives quietly. No nagging — one well-timed push at the right moment.
"Still interested in the warehouse rave? Tickets go fast — 3 of you said yes."
🫸
The gentle shove
If 3 of 5 have confirmed and 2 haven't, those 2 get a private nudge — not from the group, from the app. No public pressure.
"3 of your friends have already got their tickets for Saturday."
It works for every friend group, not just "let's go out" ones

Brainrot, political reels, dark humour —
that's taste data too.

A lot of groups don't mainly send event content. They send each other chaos memes, political satire, dark comedy, throwback stuff, aesthetic videos. That's still a signal. It's a portrait of the group's identity — their humour register, their energy, what they find interesting. Sendit reads all of it.

So for a group that's more chaotic than "let's find a restaurant" — the suggestions look different. A debate dinner. A tier list session seeded from things they've actually been referencing. A cult film night matched to their humour. A documentary that lines up with the political threads they keep sending each other.

The group persona
Over time the board builds a picture of who the group actually is — not just what they want to do next weekend, but their whole identity as a friend group. "The Chaotic Intellectuals. Equally likely to send you a political essay or a Shrek meme." That becomes a shareable card. It's the screenshot that gets sent to the group about itself.
It's not just meetups and events

Things the group owns together.

The taste graph doesn't just suggest plans — it creates things. Shared objects that belong to the group and get richer over time.

🎵
The group playlist
A Spotify playlist that regenerates weekly from the music signals in the group's reels. Not anyone's personal taste — the intersection. "The Summer Friends playlist." Nobody made it, everyone recognises it.
📜
The group manifesto
After enough reels, the AI writes a short character study of the group — their humour, their energy, their contradictions. Not a list of interests, a portrait. Shareable. Regenerates as the taste graph evolves.
⚡
The weekly debate brief
For groups that send political or philosophical content — a structured brief every Sunday based on the topics they've been sending each other. The motion, the arguments on each side, a few provocations. Designed to spark a conversation that already wanted to happen.
"This week's motion: 'We should all move back home after graduation.' Based on the 6 rent crisis reels you sent each other."
📖
The plans you never took
An archive of every idea the group sent each other but never acted on. Not as guilt — as rediscovery. "From 4 months ago — you wanted to do a rooftop dinner. Still want to?" The graveyard of almost-plans becomes a source of future ones.
After the night happens

The night doesn't disappear into
everyone's camera roll.

Right now when a plan actually happens, photos scatter across five different camera rolls, the group chat moves on, and three weeks later nobody can find the video from that rooftop. The memory exists only in people's heads, getting fuzzier every month.

When enough people commit to a plan on Sendit, the app automatically creates an event page — pre-filled with everything already known: the reel that started it, the date, the venue, who was going. It's waiting before the night even happens. After the night, it becomes the container for everything the group adds.

📸
Shared photo drop
A 48-hour window after the event where everyone drops their photos and videos into the event page — not the group chat. They live here permanently, labelled, findable.
✍️
One-line memory
Each person gets one optional prompt: "One thing you'll remember about tonight?" Five lines from five people becomes a complete picture of the night.
"Tom: the queue was worth it" · "Priya: that last hour on the terrace"
🗓
Group timeline
Every night the board turns into a real plan builds a scrollable timeline. A year of nights together — what you sent each other, what you actually did, what you said about it. The longer you use it, the more irreplaceable it becomes.
The bigger picture — don't need to explain this to teammates today

Sendit is the desire layer.
Noosh is the execution layer.

Sendit and Noosh are the same thesis at two different levels. Sendit reads what a group wants — any plan, any activity. Noosh executes one specific type of plan: the dinner party.

When a group's board fills up with food reels, recipe content, aesthetic dinner videos — Sendit can hand off to Noosh. Theme pre-filled from the signals. Dish suggestions pulled from the reels they've been sending each other. The host isn't guessing what their friends want — they already know, because Sendit has been reading it.

The full loop
Sendit — everything a group fantasises about together, turned into plans that happen.
Noosh — when one of those plans is a dinner party, this is how it gets made.

Eventually they're one product. Sendit is the container. Noosh is the dinner party module inside it.
